### Part 1 - Viewing Preferences

#### What was heard?
- In the UK:
  - Doesn't have access to TV license / sports channels.
  - Uses free online streaming sites like "Total Sportek" and "VIP Box".
- Mostly uses pirated streams (broadcasts from Migu); also Migu and Dongqiudi's Chinese football news and fan engagement, often described as the "Chinese version of OneFootball" when in China.
- Uses Instagram for short-form visual recaps, such as match highlights and specific moments (e.g. reels).
- In the UK:
  - Can't access Mexican Sky Go without a VPN.
  - Watches on Facebook or TikTok lives.
  - Sometimes uses websites that stream it.
- Uses multiple platforms to gather information about football, each with a different focus and without duplication. She is more concerned about the efficiency of using each platform and needs the platforms to deliver content accurately.
- They all watch on their own laptops or mobiles, so online platforms work better.
- "I usually watch on iPad or laptop for the bigger screen. If I'm outside, I use my phone. I just watch passively, maybe do some chores like cleaning while watching."
- Devices used:
  - Primarily a laptop or mobile phone.
  - When watching with friends: connect to TV via HDMI or casting.
- Devices:
  - Smartphone
  - Projector or screen casting to a larger display (e.g. TV or monitor)
- "I don't send live comments; I mostly just rate players or comment in the discussion section."
- Usually after the game, or maybe during halftime, but not during the actual game.
  - Updates each other (friends) about scores.
  - Shares memes about the match from Instagram.
- Interactive method:
  - No pop-ups or public comments
  - Chatting privately with friends (e.g. WeChat discussions)
  - Watching important matches with friends offline (going to a pub together)
- NO:
  - Expensive fees (compared to domestic platforms)
  - Already purchased membership on domestic platforms (e.g. Aiqiyi)
- "If official sites had good offers, especially for main months like May (Champions League main matches), maybe people might buy a subscription for that specific month, if not the whole year."
- SkySport Subscription - would have gotten one if it were cheaper.
- Sky Sports is viewed as the most reliable and comprehensive football platform.
- Appreciates Sky Sport's pre-match discussions, expert analysis, and the ability to follow a full season of content.
- "Sky Sports is solid in terms of quality, but not significantly better than other platforms to justify the high cost."
- Lots of pop-ups, even with Brave browser.
- Spoilers on Instagram from people talking about events before he sees them on the stream.
- "Bullet comments cover the pitch, sometimes I can't even see the ball."
- "Ads during live matches really ruin the experience."
- Turned off bulletchat completely to avoid negativity and visual distractions. Didn't mention changing platforms.
- No major complaints, but occasional dissatisfaction with the quality of commentary.
- "But if those two are commentating, I might watch a match just because of them. Their knowledge is deep, and they share lots of insights. That's a big reason I stick with Migu."

#### What it Means
- Users prioritise "free + accessible" over official channels.
- Users are able to differentiate between the advantages of different platforms and get the information they want from different platforms with a clear purpose.
- Users will choose their device based on where they are watching (home / outdoor) and how important the game is.
- Most interactive behaviours take place in WeChat or social groups, rather than public platforms.
- Most users tend to watch others speak but not post themselves on public platforms.
- Sky Sports is widely recognised as a high-quality platform, but usage is not high.
- Cost / price is the biggest deterrent, especially for students and existing subscribers.
- Users favour "borrowed experiences", such as in a pub or at a friend's house.
- Users generally dislike pop-up adverts and pop-ups, as they affect the viewing experience and block the screen.
- The quality of commentary has a direct impact on the choice of platform; users care about whether the commentator is professional and able to analyse tactics and techniques.

#### Why it matters
- Clear navigation should be provided according to content type to help users improve information access efficiency.
- Screen size and clarity significantly influence device selection.
- Users prefer a pure viewing experience to maintain immersion. Do not participate in the live comments section or pop-ups.
- Users trust small, familiar communication environments.
- Users are cautious about expressing themselves on public platforms.
- Sky Sports despite its high brand awareness, is actually under-utilised by Gen Z, limited mainly by price.
- Gen Z favours flexible, low-threshold content consumption methods. Preference for "short-term use" / "pay-as-you-go".
- Users would rather switch off interaction than be affected by pop-ups, and platforms need to make adjustments to the quality and size of pop-ups.
- Different users have a clear preference for commentator and can be offered different style options.

#### Findings
- Provide users with a unified platform to access multiple forms of media to help improve information access efficiency.
- Need to have a consistent interface across devices.
- The user interface needs to change slightly depending on the size of the device.
- Allowing users to engage in more personalised communities based on their preferences and friend circles will enhance their live stream engagement and expression.
- Set comment permissions, visible only to friends.
- Providing users with flexible subscription plans can increase their content consumption on the app.
- Providing the platform as a way for them to engage in social settings can provide added value and loyalty to the app.
- Reducing the number of ad pop-ups will create a better viewing experience.
- Users prefer varied commentary style. It is one of the main focus points of football fans. Providing commentary preferences and style options can better enhance the viewing experience.
- Changing the advert / pop-up format.

### Part 2 - Content Preferences and Fan Experience

#### What was heard?
- For football: Fantasy Premier League app: has used for 4-5 years; involves making a team, joining a friends' group, and competing on points based on real player performances. This is not real money betting.
- For football: 365 Scores app: for all sports, not just football; provides live updates. This app allows for betting.
- "Dongqiudi's recommendations are usually spot on - they push major news."
- Uses BBC Sport primarily to check match results, standings, and football news rather than to spend extended time on the platform.
- YouTube - watches a platform (possibly NBC or similar) with "football gurus" (ex-players) discussing players and the game in a chill way.
- Pays close attention to player positioning, team formations, and whether players are performing as expected.
- Interested in match statistics, such as possession and shots on goal, especially during halftime.
- Key to attracting him:
  - Emotional significance (player farewells)
  - Importance of strategy and performance
  - Historical points and identity transitions
- During halftime, sometimes watches commentators discuss the game and make predictions.
- Watches with friends offline and makes predictions together.
- Definitely short videos.
- Enjoys edited videos focusing on one specific player (e.g. best goals, best dribbles like Messi's top ten dribbles).
- He uses OneFootball to consume football content and says it's very personalised to his taste.
- Uploads Insta stories (mostly private stories to close friends).
- Articles:
  - Doesn't have the patience to read long articles unless they have titles that quickly grab her attention.

#### What it Means
- Users have different app preferences when looking at different forms of media.
- In match statistics, scores, match tactics, and player performances matter.
- Likes to make score predictions during halftime.
- Both men and women like short videos with highlights, but women are less inclined to report on too much text.

#### Why it matters
- Users will switch back and forth between different apps to use them, resulting in lower efficiency and inconvenience.
- Spectators will frequently view this information when watching the game, and it will be inconvenient to view this information when it is not on the game screen.
- During non-match periods (halftime), users may be interested in other information about the match.
- Men and women have different needs, and both needs have to be considered in order to be more appealing to users.

#### Findings
- Football app preferences:
  - The Fantasy Premier League app is popular for creating teams and competing based on player performance.
  - 365 Scores is used for betting.
  - OneFootball and Dongqiudi are used to watch matches and get information and advice.
  - BBC Sport is used for results and standings rather than extended browsing.
  - Users watch match highlights on YouTube and TikTok.
- Display personalised information that users find most important in the live match screen, such as score, player information, match trends, statistics, and possession.
- During breaks in the game, add options for engagement such as score prediction and similar interactions to increase the fun of the game experience during breaks.
- Ensure that short videos, highlights, and long and short text content blocks are clearly represented based on personal preferences of users.

### Part 3 - What Does Personalisation Mean to Them?

#### What was heard?
- Wants the platform to know she prefers fun, digestible content.
- "It's best not to offend any players - you know, relationships between football fans can be really complicated. Features shouldn't provoke conflict or arguments among fans. You don't want to design something that just turns into a battleground for fan fights."
- "Some fans look down on those who only watch major tournaments - like World Cup fans who appear every four years, they will say that those people don't know about football."
- Watches mainly for fun, not for learning strategy.
- Avoids debates with opposing fans, becomes quiet when in mixed groups.
- Personalisation is understood as the ability to prioritise and surface content related to the team he supports.
- Desired direction:
  - For professional analysis (player performance, team tactics, etc.)
  - Support smarter data visualisation and content filtering
  - Provide fans with tools to better understand the game
- Expressed interest in a more integrated app that could:
  - Track team and player statistics over time
  - Aggregate all relevant content in one place
  - Push recommendations based on support history
- "Since I play football it's really helpful to see what players that play in my position do and see what they do tactically and practically."
- Her coach suggests matches or clips to watch to learn more.
- "A personalised experience would let me pick which stats or players to track. Maybe mark a player with a special color in the UI during live games."
- Real Madrid member - they send a personalised birthday message. "I don't know how they did it because it was like the players, the coaches, the stadium with your name and facts about you."
- "I don't comment much on Hupu myself, maybe one or two matches. But I don't hesitate to post on Moments if a feature that post among relative close people. I think if you enjoy a game, just share it. No need to be professional - being happy watching is enough."
- Prefers watching with friends who support the same team.
- Rarely posts football-related content.
- Doesn't want to be misinterpreted as an extreme fan.
- Uploads Insta stories (mostly private stories to close friends).
- "Clubs catering to their fanbase based on what fans ask for, to keep them engaged."
- Would like more options for commentators, sound, or camera angles (like in F1 where you can choose pilot's view or follow a specific player).
- Users not only want to see content related to their team, but they also want to set tracing dimensions, such as specific players, tactics, injuries, and influence the recommendation algorithm.
- They want to control what they see during the game.
- Users do not want to rely on manual search, reflecting a strong demand shift from passive reception to active access.

#### What it Means
- High needs for "fun interaction" and interesting interaction during the game.
- Hope for a more harmonious environment (for different fans knowledge level) to avoid conflict.
- Key expectation for personalization:
  - They can focus on their favourite team / player.
  - They learn from players who play their position in the game.
- There's a certain threshold to being recognized as a "real football fan."
- Why?
  - They want to be recognised in the fanbase.
  - Fans may fear being dismissed with phrases like "Don't speak if you don't know football."
  - Football is not just about content consumption; it is a point of connection for identity and social relationships. Supporting a team is often also a form of social labelling and self-definition.
  - Knowing the game becomes a way for them to establish their self-worth in a familiar field.
- For users, "personalization" is the right to control and express.

#### Why it matters
- Reduce the "knowledge anxiety" for new fans and lower the participation threshold.
- Satisfy the need of social connection during the game with other fans and their friends.
- Personalised content needs to satisfy their "team affiliation" as fans.
- Giving fans a sense that they're more knowledgeable can boost their motivation to speak up and participate actively.

#### Insight
- Users seek fun and engaging interactions during the game, not only for entertainment but also to reduce knowledge anxiety. By lowering the cognitive barrier, platforms can encourage broader participation from casual or new fans who may otherwise feel excluded.
- Users desire a more harmonious and inclusive social environment during games, one that supports connection while respecting different levels of fan knowledge to avoid conflict or exclusion.
- Personalised content must reflect users' team affiliation, allowing them to follow their favourite team or player, and even learn from those who share their position, to deepen identification and engagement.
- For many fans, knowing football is a form of social identity and self-worth, providing them with the confidence to speak up and be recognised within the fan community.
- Personalization is not just about content relevance, it's about giving users the feeling of control, for example, control over what they track and see. And allowing them to actively shape their experience as a form of self-expression.

### Part 4 - Concerns Regarding AI

#### What was heard?
- "No, not really."
- "I feel like all my info is already out there, I don't really care anymore."
- As an ordinary person, the ability to control the risk of AI leakage is limited.
- Biggest fear: privacy leaks, especially cross-platform conversations.
- "It's scary like putting so much into the Internet where, like anyone can access it."
- Fear of software "listening in the background" even after exiting the application.
- Worried about AI continuously recording location information, behavioural paths, etc.
- Unaware of how data is being used, stored, and shared.
- Worried that AI-generated content can appear overly convincing, making it difficult to distinguish truth from falsehood.
- Mentioned the risk of AI bots reinforcing misleading narratives in online discussions.
- Doesn't fully trust AI-generated content (e.g. errors in news).
- Worried that the ideas she shared with the AI might be used elsewhere.
- The AI may provide inaccurate or fabricated information.
- The AI often "agrees with everything" rather than providing an objective or critical viewpoint.
- Wouldn't mind AI use, thinks it's good.
- Wouldn't like it to be run just by AI because current AI is very detectable.
- Prefers a mix of AI (for improving technology, making processes faster) and the "warmth of the human touch."
- Not very concerned about sharing email because he has a secondary email for such things.
- Location and contact details are more concerning.
- Willing to share:
  - Browsing history
  - Viewing habits
  - Favorite teams / players
  - Types of posts viewed
  - Location information

#### What it Means
- There is a clear divide in people's attitudes towards AI's privacy breaches: some feel powerless or numb, others have a strong sense of unease and guardedness.
- Users are generally skeptical about the credibility of AI content, fearing that they will be misled and manipulated, and that AI lacks human judgement.
- The AI's responses were seen as overly agreeable or generalised, lacking depth, critical or humane reasoning.
- Users have different privacy boundaries for different types of data, with high tolerance for behavioural data and low tolerance for identity data.

#### Why it matters
- AI products need to be designed to cater for different privacy-sensitivity groups of people - raising awareness of risk for some, and providing safeguards and trust for others.
- Trust is a prerequisite for AI to be used consistently; once trust is lost, users may actively avoid or question the platform.
- Lack of critical or personalised AI feedback can erode trust and engagement, and users may come to see AI as an "empty" tool, relying less on it or actively bypassing it.
- A one-size-fits-all data collection strategy can lead to the loss of some users and needs to be finely controlled.

#### Findings
- Users' concerns about AI privacy breaches show a clear trend of polarisation: some users are indifferent or powerless to privacy breaches, while others are highly sensitive and wary.
- Users are generally concerned about the authenticity and credibility of AI-generated content. In particular, they are worried that the content is too authentic but lacks accuracy and criticality, making it easily misleading.
- Users perceive that AI-generated content is often too general and lacks in-depth analysis, critical perspective, and a personalised human touch, leading to reduced trust and interest in AI feedback.
- Users' privacy sensitivity to different types of data varies significantly, with a tendency to share behavioural data (e.g. browsing history) more leniently, and to show stronger resistance to data involving identifying information (e.g. contact information, address).

#### Insights
- Designing a layered privacy control system and transparent data management mechanism to cater for different privacy-conscious user groups can enhance the security of sensitive users as well as gradually guide low-sensitive users to be more privacy-conscious.
- By establishing a dual verification mechanism that combines an AI content credibility rating system with fact checking, the platform can improve content credibility and strengthen users' trust in the reliability of the platform's information.
- Enhance user engagement and trust by designing AI-driven feedback that feels more human, to improve the quality and personalisation of feedback content.
- Constructing a data classification and authorisation management system that gives users control over all types of data to be shared can effectively enhance users' trust and inspire more willingness to share data.

### Part 5 - Social Engagement Dynamics

#### What was heard?
- "Oh, absolutely. It's always fun to watch with more people, and especially when people are supporting the team that you want to see lose... It becomes very exciting."
- Watching with others makes cheering and reacting feel more natural and enjoyable.
- Watch matches with others whenever possible. Likes to react with her friends with facial expressions, gestures or shouting.
- Motivation for watching: not because of the team itself, but socially driven = curiosity, feeling like you're "joining in the fun", "watching what's hot."
- "If friends recommend a match - like Bundesliga or Ligue 1 - I'll check it out, even if I don't usually watch those leagues."
- Yes. Even if she originally decides not to watch, if others around her are watching (or a match is on in front of her), she almost always gets drawn in.
- Will watch games with friends at the pub or other social settings when there is collective excitement.

#### What it Means
- Users prefer to watch football with others as it enhances their live viewing experience through small things like cheering, joking messages, and even competitive banter with rival team supporters.
- Users get influenced by friends to watch matches beyond their favourite teams. This is often out of communal excitement and can deepen their engagement with the sport.

#### Why it matters
- Watching together with friends becomes a source of tradition, intensifies excitement and increases participation, emotional investment, and even platform loyalty.
- Having more friends to come watch together will enhance their live watching experience and increase enjoyment for all.

#### Findings
- Creating exciting and engaging social experiences can elevate fans' live viewing experience.
- Offering personalised suggestions on app for watching live matches at pubs, based on user preferences and friends' activity, can enhance their live viewing engagement.
